  • The terms intraamniotic infection and chorioamnionitis are often used interchangeably however, this is a complicated topic because chorioamnionitis includes inflammation as well as infection.
  • Inflammation leads to tissue edema, swelling, and irritation. Infection includes inflammation and invasion of bacteria, virus, fungus, or other infectious agents.
Signs of chorioamnionitis include:
  • Maternal temperature greater than 38°C (100.4°F)
Plus one or more of the following:
  • Fetal tachycardia > 160 beats per minute,
  • Uterine tenderness,
  • Purulent amniotic fluid, or
  • Purulent discharge from the cervical os

Some obstetric experts now use the terminology: intrauterine inflammation, or infection or both, abbreviated Triple I (eye) instead of chorioamnionitis.
Suspected or confirmed Triple I criteria include a maternal fever with one or more of the following:

  • Fetal tachycardia, > 160 beats per minute for 10 minutes or longer
  • Maternal white blood cell count greater than 15,000 in the absence of corticosteroids
  • Confirmation of microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity by amniotic fluid culture and/or gram stain and biochemical analysis.
Intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis can be appropriately started for suspected or confirmed Triple I with reassessment of the necessity to continue antibiotics once the infant is delivered.
