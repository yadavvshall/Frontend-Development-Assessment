Less than 100,000 per microliter

A platelet count less than 100,000 per microliter is considered abnormal, especially if there is a downward trend. The infant should be examined for signs of bleeding, which includes oozing from puncture sites, bruising, petechiae, and gastrointestinal bleeding.

Less than 50,000 per microliter
  • A platelet count less than 50,000 per microliter (μL) is considered severe thrombocytopenia.
  • Ensure there was no platelet clumping on the CBC lab sample as this will artificially lead to a low platelet count being reported.
  • Observe the infant for any signs of bleeding and notify the infant’s care team of any concerns.
Less than 25,000 per microliter

Platelet counts less than 25,000 per microliter are dangerously low. Monitor closely for signs of bleeding and be prepared to administer a platelet transfusion if necessary.

Petechiae
Capillaries (small blood vessels) that burst under the skin and form reddish or purplish spots on the skin that appear similar to a rash. Petechiae may be a sign of an underlying hemorrhagic disorder, including infection. Petechiae are commonly present following a breech or precipitous delivery, or if the infant had a tight nuchal cord (face/neck area).
