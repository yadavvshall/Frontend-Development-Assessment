Fetal Infection – Transplacental Infection
The most common route for fetal infection is called ascending infection.
  • Bacterial from the vagina and cervix make their way into the intrauterine cavity and eventually through the amnion into the amniotic fluid. Ascending infection can occur even if the membranes are not ruptured.
  • Presence of bacteria in the amniotic may lead to infection of the fetus. The lungs may become infected because the amniotic fluid is moved in and out with normal fetal respiration. If the fetus becomes distressed in-utero and begins to gasp, infected fluid can be inhaled deeply into the lung and cause pneumonia.
  • Another route of fetal infection is from the mother’s bloodstream to the placenta. An example of transplacental infection is Rubella viral infection in the first trimester. Although rare, fetal infection can also occur if bacteria is accidentally introduced into the amniotic fluid during invasive procedures, such as amniocentesis, chorionic villus sampling, or percutaneous fetal blood sampling.
